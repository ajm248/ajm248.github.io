SUN Open Letter
Dear all,

Students Unite Now is a group of and for low-income students fighting to change financial aid policies. As such, its organizing needs to reflect the needs of low-income students. Currently, there is a lack of transparency about the organizing structure. SUN needs to be more democratic and transparent so that its members can shape our public actions and vision. Some of the changes are more general, some more specific.

These are some of the changes that need to be made:

-More group planning meetings

-There needs to be a mechanism for addressing issues and incidents within the structure, especially in instances where legal matters are involved. Chain-of-command style organizing reinforces issues that may make it more difficult for low-income students to participate in the organizing.

-Actions, particularly more risky actions, should be collaboratively planned. Instead of a push/step-up model, there should be a model where, when we have an action we want to do we brainstorm ways we can make that possible to do as a group. (An example: If there’s an ask to not cross a grocery store picket line, we would host a meeting to brainstorm ways we can all avoid going to the grocery store over break.)

-CDs should only be held during the school year, when students have access to on-campus housing and meal plans (the period between the initial CD and subsequent court dates counts as being a part of the CD).

-SUN should consult with the structure before issuing press releases about major changes (the public vision of SUN should be shaped by the students it represents).

SUN has built a name on campus for representing the issue of financial aid reform, particularly in respect to the policy of the SIC/Student Effort. It runs on the currency of stories; these stories represent our lives and can’t simply be cycled through and used when convenient; our voices need to be present in our organization. Having an undemocratic process means that often student voices on issues, and well-being in regards to actions, often get overlooked to prioritize other goals.

We ask that Students Unite Now changes these practices.
